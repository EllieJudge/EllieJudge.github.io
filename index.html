The road to hell is paved with good intentions

I studied Media and Performance at Salford University. The course was crap so I switched to English Literature. I loved this, and did pretty well... unfortunately getting a bit too big for my boots I applied to transfer to the University of Manchester. They accepted me! Which turned out to be a crying shame because once I got there I hated it and become increasingly lonely and isolated. Eventually dropping out from anxiety and depression. Up until that point though, I adored reading books and writing about wanky 17th century poets (nngghhhhhh Shakespeare I love you...)

Pictures

Aside from coding and buying too many bags, shoes and dresses I really enjoy photography and editing pictures.

Nervosa

Having struggled with eating disorders since the age of twelve I found a strange and unexpected comfort in photography and self portraits. This was the opposite of what I expected, as I have always hated photos of myself. Playing with colour and composition releases a creativity I rarely get to exercise. The photos themselves challenge the false images I have inside my head.

Django Girls, to Code Nation to Tech Returners

When I was just starting out on my tech adventure I went along to a meet-up called Django Girls. At this point I barely knew how to turn on my laptop let alone what Django was, but it sounded cool. It was there I made my first techy friend, met some lovely people... and somehow managed to win a Scholarship to study on the Master course at Code Nation!? The day I found out I'd won the compeition and would get to do their coding course for free was one of the best days of my life. I was screaming when I found out and had a skip in my steps for weeks after. Until that point I had felt stuck. Working in minimum wage jobs with little or no progression or exciting possibilities for the future. The Tech Dream had up until this point consisted of me, sitting alone at night in bars and pubs, working my way through free code camp with no one to ask or speak to when I got stuck. When I got to Code Nation though it was a shock to the system. I'd enjoyed coding by myself, quietly, with no pressure. Suddenly I was surrounded by people who had taken getting onto the course a lot more seriously than I had (I'd won it by accident really - never thinking I'd actually be accepted). And as everyone whizzed through the topics I fell behind and felt overwhelmed. All of my past insecurities and feelings of inadequacy crept up. Most days I cycled home crying. Fast forward nearly two years and even though I'm still not a Ninja coder (yet!) I have: gotten a job with a company that I love. Built a website. Won a Made in Manchester award (which is hilarious and in no way deserved or indicative of my actual tech ability, but still really nice). üòä

What I like about tech

There are lots of things I like about tech. I like coding. I've loved making this little page. But what thrills me the most is how someone like me, who was working in bars and restaurants and little (lovely) clothes shops, selling charity shop garms online - can suddenly get catapulted into a different life in less than a year. Towering buildings overlooking vast cities. Insights into some of the most influential companies in the world. This is insane. I can't believe it. Being someone with a keen interest in politics and making things "better", the ability to quizz people that create some of the most far-reaching apps and often - unfortunately - some pretty far-reaching problems, is thrilling. It feels like the potential of tech knows no bounds. And with this new found insight into this once, out of reach world, comes a great responsibility. Not only to, when the opportunity arises, challenge the way tech companies operate, but also, if I get the chance, to create something exciting, fresh and new.
